3D Fruit Bowl

Part one for this project was to get to know the basics of modelling and sculpting in Blender, by creating a simple turntable of a fruit bowl and some fruit to go with it.

I created the bowl after experimenting a bit with modelling and layering multiple objects, until I got the result of a unique flowery fruit bowl.

For the fruit I aimed to include a variety of fruit shapes (oranges, bananas and a bunch of grapes) that would settle inside the bowl and make for an interesting composition, and I sculpted a few details like the grape stem and the surface of the oranges to add some contrast between the fruit and the bowl.

This was a fun exercise on the whole to familiarise myself more with 3D modelling basics, and I'm pretty happy with the final result.

2D Character Sprite

The 2D counterpart to this exercise was to create a character sprite animation based on a certain theme, performing a series of consecutive actions. In my case, I chose to use Piskel to animate a pixel coffee cup with a pair of legs (but no arms).

Using a 32x32 pixel canvas, the first step was to come up with a design for the character that was iconic, but not too complex, while being able to perform the following 6 actions:

  • Idle
  • Walk
  • Run
  • Jump (fixed + variable height)
  • Fall
  • Die

After some of tweaking, I settled on a square-shaped cup with beady eyes, skinny legs and a thick pair of eyebrows. I kept a simple palette of 9 colours, avoiding any excessive detail to maintain the character's overall appeal.

The image you're seeing here is a sprite sheet of every single frame used to create all 6 actions, laid out from top to bottom. Each action was animated separately, focusing on fluid movement between frames (in most cases, literally) while playing around with facial expressions that seemed fit.

Lastly, I setup the sprite sheet in Unity and created the above sequence using an animation state-manager, which allowed for tweaking frame durations, looping certain actions a number of times and playing all 6 actions back to back.

Overall, I really enjoyed the process behind this exercise, especially in terms of character design, where sometimes limiting your options for detail can help with better appeal. It's also been pretty eye-opening for potential future projects on video game development, which could be worth a shot.
